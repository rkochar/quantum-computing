It turns out that the Quantum Fourier Transform can be used to perform arithmetic on quantum registers. Here, we will explore its use for performing a sum.

Implement a circuit that performs the following transformation on two registers on nn qubits:

|xn−1⟩…|x0⟩|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩↦|(x+y)n−1⟩…|(x+y)0⟩|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩|xn−1⟩…|x0⟩|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩↦|(x+y)n−1⟩…|(x+y)0⟩|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩

where (xn−1,…,x0),(yn−1,…,y0)(xn−1,…,x0),(yn−1,…,y0) are the binary representations of two integers x,yx,y and the sum is modulo 2n2n, i.e. if n=3n=3 and x=6,y=7x=6,y=7 then x+y=(13mod23)=5x+y=(13mod23)=5. This transformation can be visualized by the following circuit:


The only allowed gates are qiskit’s cp and H gates.

Hint: this exercise is asking you to implement Draper’s adder and can be summarized in three steps. Recall that the implementation of the QFT in the book makes use of swap gates, which you are not allowed to use. You can create your own swap gates using equalities, or altogether avoid the swaps.

  1. Apply a QFT to the bottom nn qubits. We can now rewrite this state as follows, using the notation of the lecture week 6 slide 16, where N=2nN=2n and ωN=ei2π/NωN=ei2π/N:
    |0⟩+ωN/2⋅xN|1⟩2–√⊗|0⟩+ωN/4⋅xN|1⟩2–√…|0⟩+ωxN|1⟩2–√|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩=|0⟩+ωNN/2⋅x|1⟩2⊗|0⟩+ωNN/4⋅x|1⟩2…|0⟩+ωNx|1⟩2|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩=

    |0⟩+ωx2|1⟩2–√⊗|0⟩+ωx4|1⟩2–√…|0⟩+ωxN|1⟩2–√|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩|0⟩+ω2x|1⟩2⊗|0⟩+ω4x|1⟩2…|0⟩+ωNx|1⟩2|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩
  2. Add yy into the exponent of ωω with controlled Z rotations. This will take the state to:
    |0⟩+ωx+y2|1⟩2–√…|0⟩+ωx+yN|1⟩2–√|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩|0⟩+ω2x+y|1⟩2…|0⟩+ωNx+y|1⟩2|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩
  3. Undo the Quantum Fourier Transform to recover x+yx+y on the bottom register:
    |(x+y)n−1⟩…|(x+y)0⟩|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩|(x+y)n−1⟩…|(x+y)0⟩|yn−1⟩…|y0⟩
